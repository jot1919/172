Meet Sydney: The Fourth-Grade Journal Keeper

Goals
  • Write Freely & Openly: Seeks a welcoming space to share her thoughts without judgment or pressure.
  • Develop Self-Awareness: Hopes to learn about her emotions early, so she can manage them more effectively later in life.
  • Improve Communication: Wants to feel comfortable expressing how she feels, both in writing and potentially with others.
Frustrations
  • Lack of Structure: A blank journal can be intimidating—Sydney struggles with what to write if there's no prompt.
  • Variable Depth: She finds it hard to uncover deeper insights without guidance or reflection questions.
  • Forgetting to Contribute: Without regular reminders, she often forgets to add new entries, losing the habit quickly.
Background

Sydney is a 4th grader in California whose parents encouraged her to journal her emotions. They believe this will help her develop healthier coping skills. Although she values their support, Sydney becomes frustrated when she doesn't know what to write about, leading her to abandon the journal for days or even weeks. She wants to stay consistent but needs prompted guidance and occasional reminders to keep up a meaningful habit.

Meet Zoe Clark: The Imaginative 5th Grader

Bio

Zoe is a curious and creative 10-year-old who loves reading and drawing. Though she's comfortable with technology, she finds it difficult to communicate her emotions openly—especially with adults. Interactive or game-like apps initially grab her attention, but she loses interest if there's not enough variety or encouragement to keep going.

Core Needs
  • Fun Emotional Outlets: A playful, journal-style platform that doesn't feel like extra homework.
  • Reminders & Streaks: Regular nudges or incentives that help her maintain the habit.
  • Privacy & Security: A safe personal space to express herself without fear of judgment.
Frustrations
  • Habit Formation: Struggles to stay consistent without tangible rewards or milestones.
  • Shyness: Feels uncomfortable discussing emotions directly with parents or teachers.
  • Engagement: Quickly grows bored if an app lacks variety, interactive prompts, or gamification features.

Final Project Report

Want to learn more about our research and findings? We invite you to explore our Final Project Report, where we share a comprehensive look at how this project took shape. Inside, you'll find:

Key Interview Insights

We conducted interviews with both children and early adolescents, discovering common barriers like journaling consistency, lack of engaging prompts, and minimal emotional awareness.

Literature-Based Rationale

Drawing on studies in mental health informatics, we highlight the importance of early intervention to prevent issues like anxiety or depression from escalating into adulthood.

Parental Involvement Strategies

We detail how minimal but meaningful oversight—like calendar check-ins—helps maintain consistency without breaching a child's privacy.

Prototype Evaluations

Feedback on our low-fidelity app wireframes helped refine our approach, ensuring features like short, daily prompts and easy navigation speak directly to a child's needs.

Together, these findings emphasize a clear gap in accessible, child-friendly mental health solutions, reinforcing why we designed a platform that blends playful interaction with essential emotional support tools.
